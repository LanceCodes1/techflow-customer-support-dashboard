# Case Study: SmartInventory - Preventing Stock Shortages at QuickMart Grocery
## Business Issue: The Empty Shelf Problem

### Company
QuickMart (a fictional neighborhood grocery chain)

### Problem
Frequent stock shortages on popular items, especially during weekends and holidays, leading to lost sales and frustrated customers who switch to competitors.

### Customer Impact Example:
Maria plans her weekly family dinner and visits QuickMart on Saturday at 6 PM for ingredients. The ground beef, pasta sauce, and milk are all sold out. She leaves frustrated, purchases everything at a competitor, and decides to shop elsewhere going forward.

### Root Cause Hypothesis:
Inventory is reordered based on a fixed schedule rather than actual sales patterns, and management doesn't track how quickly specific items sell at different times.

## Project Solution: Sales Pattern Analysis System
### Objective
Generate and analyze simulated sales data to identify which items sell fastest and when, enabling data-driven inventory decisions. Complete this for at least a 90-day period.

### Tools for Beginner Programmers:
- Python with `Faker` library: To generate realistic fake call data
- `pandas`: For data organization
- Python's `random` library: For number methods
- `datetime`: For time values
- Lovable (plotting library): To create clear visualizations showing pattern

## Required Data
You need to effectively capture `product` and `sales` information, so you will require the following pieces of information:
### Product
* Product ID (UUID)
* Product Name (String)
* Categories of products: `Dairy`, `Meat`, `Produce`, `Bakery`, `Beverages`, `Frozen` (Random array index)
* Base sale rate (Integer)
* Day of the week the sale took place (Date - Day Format)
* A weekend muliplies/booster (Decimal Number)

### Sales
* Sales date (Date)
* Day of the week (Date - Day format)
* Product ID (borrowed from `Product`)
* Product name (borrowed from `Product`)
* Category (borrowed from `Product`)
* Units sold (use `base sale rate` from `Product` but convert to integer)
* Weekend tracker (Boolean)

## How to Use the Data
1. Create a function that does the following:
    1. Create a way to store multiple pieces of data in `products`
    2. Create a way to define your `categories` for later use
    3. Generates at least 50 records *iteratively* to store in `products`
        1. In doing so, assign the product a random `category` using `random`
        2. As the function iteratively generate new records, the `product` should add new pieces of data to our `products` table, including a product ID that incremements by `1` with each new entry, product name generated by `faker` (with a random label of `"Premium"`, `"Organic"`, `"Classic"`, or `"Fresh"`), a random category, base sale rate integer determined by `random` with a minimum of `5` and a maximum of `40`, and a weekend multiplier uniformally distributed between `1.2` and `2.5`
    4. Create a way to store multiple pieces of data in `sales`
    5. Create a starting date for sales collection, using `datetime`
    6. Over a **90 day** period, the function should determine:
        1. The current date, offset relative to the starting date
        2. What day of the week it is, based on the current date
        3. Per each product on any given day of the week, generate a `daily_sales` value based on the product's base sale rate (how many items of a product are sold in a day) with a uniform distribution using `random`'s `uniform` method, with a low of `0.7` and a high of `1.3`
        4. Per each product, determine how much the sales boost is worth depending on the day of the week (i.e, Saturday and Sunday include a weekend boost) using the product's `weekend_multiplier`
        5. Per each product, determine how much the sales boost is worth depending on the month of the year (i.e, July and December include a holiday boost) with a uniform distribution using `random`'s `uniform` method, with a low of `1.3` and a high of `1.8`
        6. After all of the above is evaluated, the `sales` should add new pieces of data to our `sales` table, including the current day, the day of the week in day format (i.e. "Monday, "Tuesday", etc), product ID pulled from the `product` table, product name pulled from the `product` table, number of units sold (daily sales converted to an integer), and if the day the sale took place was a weekend day
2. Create a dataframe with `Pandas` to create a basic representation of the `sales` table
3. Use `pandas` to export a `.csv` file to be used in Lovable in the next section
4. Resolve any errors arising in the terminal as you develop your project
